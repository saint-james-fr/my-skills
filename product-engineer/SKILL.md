# Product Engineer Mindset

Principles distilled from Ami Vora's career at Meta (Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp) and "The Hard Parts of Growth" — for engineers who think like product owners.

---

## The 3 Key Jobs of Building a Product

Every successful builder does these three things. Product engineers own all three.

1. **Recognize the customer problem** — Identify it from research, observations, or strategy. Build conviction it's the most important problem to solve now. Develop a radar for what users struggle with.

2. **Structure a solution** — Hypotheses, target audience, milestones, success criteria. Frame micro-decisions with clear principles. Turn a general problem into an actionable solution.

3. **Execute on the solution** — Make it ship. Create urgency and accountability. Divide work, document, pick up leftovers. *"Flow to wherever the water is lowest"* — whatever the team can't cover, you carry.

Repeat on bigger, harder problems each time.

---

## Execution Beats Strategy

- **Perfect strategy + poor execution** = you lose, and you learn nothing (was it strategy or execution?).
- **Mediocre strategy + great execution** = you lose, but you know why. Update strategy, ship again. Eventually you win.

**Implication:** Spend ~20% on strategy, ~80% on execution. Ship fast, get customer feedback, iterate. You get many tries — use them.

---

## Ownership and Initiative

**If you can see a problem, it's yours to solve.** You're likely best positioned; others may not even see it.

- Take perfection off the table — propose imperfect solutions. Progress today beats waiting for perfect.
- Think of learning as the reward.
- Ask: "What are you doing today that I can take off your plate?" to find high-leverage work.

---

## Bring Recommendations, Not Just Information

Your manager owns context; you own the recommendation. You're closer to the work — don't dump slides and expect them to decide.

**Frame work as:**
- Clear recommendation with reasoning
- Decision criteria and tradeoffs
- What you need from them: complementary context, frameworks, pattern-matching, or confidence

---

## Prioritization Until It Hurts

Prioritization means cutting *valuable* things to double down on bigger goals. If you're not disappointed by your cut list, you're not cutting hard enough.

**Rule:** Don't say no to many small things. Say YES to a few big ones, then block time. Whatever doesn't fit gets delegated or dropped.

---

## Hard Decisions Have No Perfect Solutions

- Get comfortable with imperfection. Form opinions fast enough to unblock.
- Define guiding principles for this role (e.g., "What helps X users most in 3–5 years?").
- When communicating: summarize options, acknowledge the difficulty, explain why your principles led to this choice. People can disagree but should see their voice was heard.

---

## Making Progress on Controversial Problems

When everyone has an opinion and no one agrees:

1. **Know where you are** — Info-gathering (expand) vs. solution-narrowing (contract). Don't propose answers before you have baseline facts.
2. **Use documents** — Write it down. See the obvious questions. Separate facts from assumptions.
3. **Over-communicate** — Share process and status. "This week I'm talking with X, Y, Z; Monday I'll share a recommendation."
4. **Write an opinionated recommendation** — Summary, info learned, goals, options with pros/cons, why this one, next steps. Don't hold out for perfect.

---

## Growth Axes: Complexity, Autonomy, Throughput

Improve in this order for any problem type:

- **Complexity** — How hard a problem can you solve?
- **Autonomy** — Can you handle it with minimal guidance? (Pull in manager when needed, not by default.)
- **Throughput** — How many hard problems at once?

Build systems (e.g., weekly status → published notes) to free capacity for more problems.

---

## Product Vision (When Scaling)

- Be a **facilitator**, not sole creator. Ideas from everywhere.
- Get diverse inputs: data, research, leadership opinion, your own.
- Write short, provocative drafts. Extreme enough to get pointed feedback.
- Appeal to the whole team: user story, data, challenge, how it fits.

---

## Quick Checklist for Product Engineering

When building or deciding:

- [ ] Am I solving the right customer problem? Do I have conviction?
- [ ] Is my solution structured with clear principles and success criteria?
- [ ] Am I biasing toward execution and shipping over more strategy?
- [ ] Am I bringing a recommendation with reasoning, not just data?
- [ ] Have I prioritized ruthlessly? (Does my cut list hurt?)
- [ ] Am I owning this problem or waiting for permission?
- [ ] If controversial: do we agree on facts before solutions?
